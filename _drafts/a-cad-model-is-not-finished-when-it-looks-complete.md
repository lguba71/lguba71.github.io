---
title: A CAD Model Is Not Finished When It Looks Complete
categories: [Engineering, Design]
tags: [cad, mechanical-design, manufacturability]
---

A CAD model can look complete on the screen and still be unfinished from an engineering point of view.

The geometry may be there.

The main shape may be correct.

The part may even look clean and professional.

But a useful CAD model has to do more than represent geometry.

It has to support manufacturing, assembly, inspection, communication, and future modification.

![A CAD model is not finished when it looks complete](/assets/img/posts/cad-model-not-finished-when-it-looks-complete.png)

A model is not truly finished if important features are hard to understand.

It is not finished if the design intent is unclear.

It is not finished if later changes will easily break the model.

It is not finished if manufacturing still has to guess what is important.

It is not finished if the drawing cannot be created clearly from it.

A finished CAD model should be understandable for the next person in the process.

That person may be another engineer.

It may be a CAM programmer.

It may be a supplier.

It may be an assembly technician.

It may be someone who has to modify the design months or years later.

Before calling a model finished, I like to check:

- is the geometry manufacturable?
- are the important features easy to understand?
- are references and relationships controlled intentionally?
- can the model be modified without unnecessary risk?
- does the model support a clear drawing?
- does it reduce questions for manufacturing and assembly?

A good CAD model is not only a digital shape.

It is a practical engineering tool.

It should make the next steps easier, not create additional uncertainty.
